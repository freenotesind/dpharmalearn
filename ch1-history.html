Chapter 1 — Pharmaceutics I

History & Development of Pharmacy

History of pharmacy profession in India · Pharmacy education, industry & practice · Professional associations · Pharmacy as a career · Pharmacopoeia — IP, BP, USP, NF & Extra Pharmacopoeia

1. Introduction & Definition of Pharmacy

Pharmacy is the science and art of preparing, compounding, dispensing and proper utilisation of drugs for prevention, diagnosis and treatment of disease. The word pharmacy comes from the Greek word pharmakon meaning drug, medicine or poison.

A Pharmacist is a health professional who is an expert in medicines — their preparation, dispensing, actions, uses, interactions and side effects.

Key Definitions

  • Pharmacy: Science and art of preparing, compounding and dispensing drugs
  • Pharmacist: A registered health professional expert in medicines
  • Pharmacopoeia: Official book of drug standards published by a government or authorised body
  • Pharmacology: Study of drugs and their effects on living systems
  • Pharmacognosy: Study of drugs of natural origin (plants, animals, minerals)

2. History of Pharmacy Profession in India

2.1 Ancient Period — Ayurvedic Pharmacy (Before 600 BCE)

Pharmacy in India has its roots in Ayurveda — the ancient Indian system of medicine. The word Ayurveda comes from Sanskrit: ayur = life and veda = knowledge.

  • Vedic Period (1500–600 BCE): Atharvaveda contains references to medicinal plants and their uses. This is considered the earliest evidence of pharmacy practice in India.
  • Charaka Samhita (1000–900 BCE): Written by Charaka — describes internal medicine with 341 plant drugs, 177 animal drugs and 64 mineral drugs.
  • Sushruta Samhita (600 BCE): Written by Sushruta — deals with surgery and describes 395 medicinal plants. Sushruta is known as the Father of Indian Surgery.
  • Tridosha Theory: Ayurveda is based on the theory of three doshas — Vata (air/space), Pitta (fire/water) and Kapha (water/earth). Balance of these doshas = good health.

2.2 Medieval Period (600 CE – 1757 CE)

  • Unani System: Introduced by Arabs during Muslim rule. Based on the teachings of Avicenna (Ibn Sina). Uses drugs of plant, animal and mineral origin.
  • Siddha System: Practiced in South India (Tamil Nadu). Uses mainly mineral drugs and plant drugs.
  • During this period, Vaidyas (practitioners of Ayurveda) and Hakeems (practitioners of Unani) served as both physician and pharmacist.

2.3 British Period (1757–1947)

  • The East India Company introduced western medicine and pharmacy to India.
  • First medical college established in Calcutta in 1835.
  • The Pharmacy Act 1948 (enacted 1940) was the first legislation to regulate pharmacy practice in India.
  • The Drugs and Cosmetics Act 1940 was enacted to regulate manufacture, import and distribution of drugs.
  • The Drugs Control Act 1950 further strengthened drug control.

2.4 Post-Independence Period (1947 onwards)

  • Pharmacy Council of India (PCI) established in 1949 under Pharmacy Act 1940.
  • First edition of Indian Pharmacopoeia (IP) published in 1955.
  • D.Pharm (Diploma in Pharmacy) course standardised by PCI across India.
  • India grew to become the world's largest supplier of generic medicines — supplying 20% of global generic drug exports.
  • Establishment of large pharmaceutical companies: Cipla (1935), Sun Pharma, Dr. Reddy's, Ranbaxy (1961).

4. Pharmacy Industry in India

India's pharmaceutical industry is one of the largest and most important in the world.

  • India is the world's largest supplier of generic medicines — 20% of global generic drug volume
  • India supplies 60% of global vaccine demand
  • India is the 3rd largest producer of drugs by volume in the world
  • India is the 14th largest by value globally
  • The Indian pharma market is worth approximately USD 50 billion (2023)
  • Major pharmaceutical hubs: Hyderabad (Bulk drugs), Mumbai (Formulations), Ahmedabad, Bangalore

5. Pharmacy Practice in India

5.1 Community / Retail Pharmacy

  • Most common area of pharmacy practice in India
  • Pharmacist dispenses prescription and OTC medicines to patients
  • Provides patient counselling on medication use, storage and side effects
  • Must be registered with the State Pharmacy Council
  • Regulated by: Drugs and Cosmetics Act 1940 and Pharmacy Act 1948

5.2 Hospital Pharmacy

  • Provides comprehensive pharmaceutical services within a hospital
  • Functions: Drug procurement, storage, dispensing, IV admixture, TPN, patient counselling, adverse drug reaction monitoring
  • Hospital pharmacist works as part of the healthcare team

5.3 Industrial Pharmacy

  • Works in pharmaceutical manufacturing companies
  • Functions: Drug formulation, quality control (QC), quality assurance (QA), regulatory affairs, production

5.4 Clinical Pharmacy

  • Patient-focused pharmacy practice — pharmacist directly involved in patient care
  • Therapeutic drug monitoring, drug interaction checking, dose optimisation
  • Requires Pharm.D qualification in India

8. Pharmacy Act 1940

Pharmacy Act 1940 — Must Know!

  • Enacted: 1940 by Parliament of India
  • Came into force: 1948
  • Purpose: To regulate the profession and practice of pharmacy throughout India
  • Ensures: Only qualified and registered pharmacists can practice pharmacy in India

9. Pharmacopoeia

Definition of Pharmacopoeia

A Pharmacopoeia is an official book published by a government or authorised body which contains a list of drugs with their standards for identity, purity, strength and quality. It also contains methods of preparation, storage conditions and tests for drugs.

9.1 Indian Pharmacopoeia (IP) — Most Important!

  • Published by: Indian Pharmacopoeia Commission (IPC), Ghaziabad, Uttar Pradesh
  • Under: Ministry of Health & Family Welfare, Government of India
  • Legal status: Official — drugs must comply with IP standards if marketed in India
  • IPC established: 1999 as an autonomous institution

9.2 Salient Features of Indian Pharmacopoeia (IP)

  • Contains standards for identity, purity, strength and quality of drugs
  • Includes monographs for drugs, excipients, dosage forms, biological products and herbal drugs
  • Contains general notices, tests, reagents, standard solutions and reference standards
  • IP reference standards prepared and distributed by IPC
  • Includes new drugs approved for use in India
  • Contains Ayurvedic, Unani and Siddha (ASU) drug monographs also
  • Available in print and digital (online) formats
  • Legal force: Any drug sold in India must conform to IP standards (Drugs & Cosmetics Act)

10. Other Important Pharmacopoeias

10.1 British Pharmacopoeia (BP)

  • Country: United Kingdom
  • Published by: British Pharmacopoeia Commission, UK
  • First published: 1864
  • Significance: One of the oldest and most respected pharmacopoeias; used internationally; includes medicines and pharmaceutical substances

10.2 United States Pharmacopoeia (USP)

  • Country: USA
  • Published by: United States Pharmacopoeial Convention (non-profit organisation)
  • First published: 1820
  • Significance: Oldest pharmacopoeia in USA; combined with National Formulary (NF) as USP-NF; widely used globally
  • USP-NF: USP contains drug and pharmaceutical standards; NF contains excipient standards

10.3 National Formulary (NF)

  • Published along with USP as USP-NF
  • Contains standards primarily for pharmaceutical excipients (inactive ingredients)
  • In India, the National Formulary of India (NFI) is published by IPC; contains therapeutic guidelines and drug information

10.4 Extra Pharmacopoeia (Martindale)

  • Full name: Martindale: The Complete Drug Reference (formerly Extra Pharmacopoeia)
  • Published by: Royal Pharmaceutical Society, UK
  • Content: Comprehensive drug reference book — contains drugs NOT in official pharmacopoeias; includes new drugs, investigational drugs, herbal medicines, diagnostics
  • Significance: NOT an official pharmacopoeia — it is a reference book; very useful for detailed drug information, doses, uses, preparations and manufacturer details
  • Commonly called: "Martindale"

Extra Pharmacopoeia vs Official Pharmacopoeia

  • Official Pharmacopoeia (IP, BP, USP): Published by government or authorised body; has legal force; contains official drug standards
  • Extra Pharmacopoeia (Martindale): NOT an official pharmacopoeia; a comprehensive reference book; no legal force; contains drugs not in official pharmacopoeias
  • Key difference: Official pharmacopoeias have legal status; Martindale is only a reference guide

Quick Revision Summary
