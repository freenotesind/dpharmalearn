Pharmaceutical Packaging & Storage

Complete notes — importance of packaging, container types (IP), packaging materials, glass types, plastics, rubber, blister packs, labelling requirements and storage conditions.

1. Introduction & Definition

Pharmaceutical packaging refers to the process of enclosing or protecting pharmaceutical products for distribution, storage, sale and use. Packaging is considered an integral part of the dosage form — it directly affects product safety, stability, efficacy and patient safety.

Definition

A container is an article that holds or is intended to contain a pharmaceutical product and is in direct contact with the product. The container and its closure together constitute the packaging system.

1.1 Functions / Importance of Packaging

  • Protection — from moisture, oxygen, light, temperature changes, mechanical damage, microorganisms and contamination
  • Containment — keeps product safely contained throughout distribution and storage
  • Identity — label carries essential information: drug name, dose, manufacturer, batch number, expiry date, storage instructions
  • Patient safety — child-resistant packaging; tamper-evident seals; unit-dose packaging reduces medication errors
  • Convenience — ease of dispensing, measuring, carrying and administration (e.g., blister packs for compliance packaging)
  • Compliance — blister/calendar packs improve patient adherence to medication schedules
  • Sterility maintenance — hermetically sealed containers maintain sterility of injectables and ophthalmic preparations

1.2 Classification of Packaging

LevelDescriptionExample
Primary packagingDirectly contacts the pharmaceutical productBlister pack (PVC tray with drug), Ampoule, HDPE tablet bottle, IV bag
Secondary packagingContains the primary package; provides additional protection and informationCardboard carton containing blister strips, Box containing vial
Tertiary packagingUsed for bulk transport and storage; group packagingCorrugated cardboard box containing many cartons, Shrink-wrapped pallets

3. Glass Packaging Materials

Glass has been the most traditional packaging material for pharmaceuticals due to its excellent properties: chemically inert, impermeable, transparent (allows visual inspection), rigid, non-porous and non-reactive with most drugs.

3.1 Types of Pharmaceutical Glass (IP Classification)

⭐ Glass Types — Must Memorise! Most Important Exam Topic!

Glass types based on chemical resistance and hydrolytic resistance:

Glass TypeCompositionChemical ResistanceHydrolytic Class (IP)Use
Type I — Borosilicate glassSiO₂ (80%) + B₂O₃ (12%) + Al₂O₃ + alkali oxides (low)Highest chemical resistance — almost inert; very low extractablesClass I (highly resistant)ALL parenteral preparations (injections, IV fluids); ophthalmic preparations; blood products; biological products — STERILE USE
Type II — Treated soda-lime glassSoda-lime glass (Na₂O-CaO-SiO₂) with sulphur-treated inner surface (dealkylised)Good chemical resistance — surface treated to remove alkaliClass II (surface treated)Parenteral solutions that are not highly sensitive; aqueous injectables with acidic or neutral pH
Type III — Soda-lime glassRegular soda-lime glass (Na₂O-CaO-SiO₂); no surface treatmentModerate — may leach alkali into solutionsClass III (general purpose)Non-parenteral liquid preparations; tablets and capsules; dry injectables (powders for injection)
Type NP (Type IV) — General purpose glassSoda-lime glass; lowest qualityLow resistance — not suitable for aqueous preparationsClass IV (NP — not for parenteral)Non-aqueous preparations only; solid dosage forms; non-pharmaceutical use

3.2 Properties of Glass

  • Chemical inertness — does not react with most drugs
  • Impermeability — absolutely impermeable to gases, moisture and liquids
  • Transparency — allows visual inspection of product clarity and particle content
  • Rigidity — maintains shape and size; good mechanical protection
  • Heat resistance — can be sterilised by autoclaving and dry heat
  • Recyclable — environmentally friendly; can be melted and reformed

3.3 Disadvantages of Glass

  • Fragility — breaks easily; risk of glass particles contaminating product
  • Heavy — expensive to transport compared to plastic
  • Leaching — Type III and NP glass may leach alkali ions into aqueous solutions (raises pH)
  • Delamination — inner glass surface can delaminate (small glass flakes in solution) — critical issue with borosilicate glass at extreme pH

3.4 Amber (Coloured) Glass

Glass coloured with iron and manganese oxides to produce amber (brown) colour. Amber glass absorbs ultraviolet and visible light (280–450 nm range) — protects photosensitive drugs from light degradation. Used for: NTG tablets, Folic acid injection, Riboflavin, Vitamin C preparations, Furosemide injection.

4. Plastic Packaging Materials

Plastics have largely replaced glass for many pharmaceutical applications due to their lightness, flexibility and impact resistance. However, plastics present challenges of sorption (drug absorbed into plastic) and migration (plastic additives leaching into product).

4.1 Types of Plastics Used in Pharmaceutical Packaging

PlasticAbbreviationPropertiesPharmaceutical Use
High Density PolyethyleneHDPERigid, opaque, excellent moisture barrier, chemical resistant, inexpensiveTablet and capsule bottles (most common); eye drop bottles; nasal spray containers
Low Density PolyethyleneLDPEFlexible, transparent, good moisture barrier, soft and squeezableSqueeze bottles; eye drop bottles; IV bags (less common); sachet films
PolypropylenePPRigid, high-temperature resistant (autoclavable at 121°C), clarity, excellent chemical resistanceSyringes; IV bags; caps and closures; ampoule-style containers; nasal sprays; inhalers
Polyvinyl ChloridePVCTransparent, flexible (with plasticiser DEHP), good clarity, heat-sealableBlister pack forming film (thermoformable); IV bags; blood bags; tubing
Polyvinylidene ChloridePVDCExcellent moisture and oxygen barrier; applied as coating on PVC filmsBlister pack films (PVC/PVDC) for moisture-sensitive drugs
Polyethylene TerephthalatePETClear, strong, lightweight, excellent gas barrier propertiesLiquid preparation bottles; water bottles; large-volume parenteral containers
Cyclic Olefin CopolymerCOC / COPUltra-pure, low extractables, transparent, excellent moisture barrier; autoclavablePrefilled syringes; vials as glass alternative; biologic drug containers

4.2 Problems with Plastic Packaging — Important!

ProblemDefinitionExampleSignificance
Sorption (Drug loss)Drug is absorbed INTO the plastic wall from the product — reduces drug concentration in formulationInsulin absorbed into PVC IV bags; nitroglycerin absorbed into PVC tubing; diazepam sorbed into PVCReduces effective drug dose; patient gets less drug than labelled
Migration (Contamination)Plastic additives (plasticisers, antioxidants, stabilisers) leach OUT of plastic INTO the productDEHP (plasticiser) from PVC IV bags migrates into IV solutions — especially with lipid solutions and blood productsDEHP is a suspected endocrine disruptor; banned for use with blood products for neonates in many countries
PermeationSmall molecules (gases, moisture, solvents) permeate through plastic wall in either directionOxygen permeates into LDPE bottles — oxidises drug; moisture permeates through PE into hygroscopic capsulesDrug oxidation; moisture uptake affecting capsule/tablet stability
LeachingSimilar to migration — plastics release residual monomers, catalysts or additives into productResidual vinyl chloride monomer from PVC; Bisphenol A (BPA) from polycarbonate bottlesToxicological concern; regulatory concern

5. Rubber Closures

Rubber closures (stoppers, bungs) are used as seals for injectable vials, IV bags and ampoules. They must allow needle penetration while maintaining sterility and seal integrity.

5.1 Types of Rubber

TypeExamplesPropertiesUse
Natural rubber (Latex)Polyisoprene (from Hevea brasiliensis)Excellent elasticity; good self-sealing after needle; may cause latex allergy; may leach extractablesHistorical use in vials; now replaced in many applications due to latex allergy concerns
Synthetic rubber — Butyl rubberIsobutylene-isoprene copolymerExcellent gas impermeability (best of all rubbers); low water vapour transmission; low extractables; good chemical resistance; low gas permeabilityMost widely used for pharmaceutical vial stoppers; preferred for biologicals and vaccines
Bromobutyl rubberBrominated butyl rubberBetter heat-resistance than butyl; excellent impermeability; low extractablesLyophilised (freeze-dried) products; products requiring high-temperature processing
Chlorobutyl rubberChlorinated butyl rubberGood chemical resistance; lower gas permeabilityMulti-dose vials; certain injectable preparations
Silicone rubberPolydimethylsiloxaneExtreme temperature range (-60°C to +250°C); chemically inert; low extractables; biocompatibleIV tubing; peristaltic pump tubing; implantable devices; specialist pharmaceutical use

5.2 Requirements for Pharmaceutical Rubber Closures

  • Must be elastic — self-sealing after needle insertion and withdrawal
  • Must be chemically inert — no reaction with drug product
  • Low extractables and leachables — rubber additives must not migrate into product
  • Impermeable to moisture, oxygen and other gases
  • Must withstand sterilisation (autoclaving at 121°C)
  • Compatible with drug product — some rubber additives adsorb certain drugs

6. Blister Packaging

Blister packs are the most widely used primary packaging for solid oral dosage forms (tablets, capsules). They consist of a forming film (blister tray) and a lidding foil.

6.1 Components of Blister Packaging

ComponentMaterialFunction
Forming film (base web)PVC (standard), PVC/PVDC (moisture-barrier), Alu/Alu (cold-form maximum protection), PVC/Aclar (PCTFE)Forms the pockets/blisters that hold individual dosage units; thermoformable (PVC) or cold-formable (Alu)
Lidding foilAluminium foil laminate (Al foil + heat-seal coating + paper or polyester)Seals over blister tray; patient pushes tablet through foil to access
Heat-seal coatingVinyl coating or polyester resin on foilBonds lidding foil to forming film under heat and pressure

6.2 Types of Blister Packaging

TypeForming FilmBarrier PropertiesUseCost
Standard blister (Thermoform)PVC alone (250 µm)Moderate moisture barrier; poor O₂ barrierStandard tablets and capsules in normal conditionsLowest
Improved moisture barrierPVC/PVDC laminateGood moisture barrier (PVDC reduces WVTR significantly)Moisture-sensitive tablets; hygroscopic drugsModerate
High barrierPVC/Aclar (PCTFE)Excellent moisture barrier; near-hermeticHighly moisture-sensitive drugs; expensive biologicalsHigh
Cold-form blister (Alu-Alu)Aluminium foil laminate (OPA/Alu/PVC) — cold-formed (no heat)Maximum protection — essentially zero moisture and oxygen transmission; complete light barrierHighly sensitive drugs; hygroscopic; light-sensitive; oxygen-sensitive drugs (e.g., certain antibiotics, steroids)Highest

⭐ Cold-Form vs Thermoform — Key Difference

Thermoform blister: Plastic (PVC) formed into blisters using heat — transparent or translucent; lower protection.
Cold-form (Alu-Alu) blister: Aluminium foil formed cold at room temperature — opaque silver; maximum moisture, oxygen and light protection; used for most sensitive drugs.

8. Storage Conditions (IP)

Improper storage is one of the main causes of drug degradation. The IP defines standard storage conditions that manufacturers must specify on labels and that pharmacies/hospitals must maintain.

8.1 Standard Storage Conditions (IP)

Condition TermTemperature RangeWhen UsedExamples
Freezer−20°C to −10°C (−4°F to 14°F)Products that degrade rapidly at higher temperatures; long-term storage of biologicalsSome vaccines (Varicella); Plasma; Certain biologicals; Ergometrine injection
Cold (Refrigerator)2°C to 8°C (36°F to 46°F)Thermolabile drugs; biologicals; vaccines; insulin; certain injectionsInsulin; Vaccines (BCG, DPT, MMR); Erythropoietin; Hepatitis B vaccine; Suppositories; Eye drops (after opening)
Cool8°C to 15°CProducts sensitive to ambient temperature but not requiring refrigeratorSome suppositories; preparations with volatile components; old pharmacopoeial products
Room temperature / Controlled room temperature15°C to 25°C (59°F to 77°F)Most oral solid dosage forms; most topical preparationsTablets, capsules, syrups, powders, ointments, creams (most)
Warm30°C to 40°CRare — some products require warmer storage (unusual)Certain emulsions; older preparations
Protect from lightAny temperature — store in light-resistant container or dark placePhotosensitive drugsNitroglycerin (GTN); Nifedipine; Folic acid; Riboflavin; Furosemide; Sodium nitroprusside
Protect from moistureKeep in tightly closed container with desiccantHygroscopic drugs; moisture-sensitive capsules; effervescent tabletsAspirin; Acetylsalicylic acid; Gelatin capsules; Effervescent vitamin C

8.2 Cold Chain

The cold chain is the unbroken temperature-controlled supply chain from manufacturer to patient for cold-storage products (especially vaccines). Any break in the cold chain (temperature excursion) can irreversibly damage heat-sensitive biologicals.

  • Vaccines must be stored at 2–8°C throughout distribution
  • Ice-lined refrigerators (ILR) and vaccine carriers with ice packs used in immunisation programmes
  • Cold chain monitoring: Vaccine Vial Monitors (VVM) on vaccine vials show temperature excursion history by irreversible colour change
  • Walk-in cold rooms for large storage; domestic refrigerators must never be used at field level

9. Special Packaging Topics

9.1 Unit-Dose Packaging

Each unit (tablet/capsule) individually packaged and labelled with full information. Advantages: reduces medication errors, prevents contamination, easier dispensing, improves traceability. Used in hospitals (unit-dose dispensing systems).

9.2 Strip Packaging vs Blister Packaging

PropertyStrip PackagingBlister Packaging
Forming filmTwo flat films heat-sealed around tabletsOne film thermoformed into blisters; lidded with foil
TransparencyOpaque or semi-transparentCan be transparent (PVC) — drug visible
MachineStrip packaging machine (simpler)Blister packaging machine (more complex)
Moisture protectionGood (sealed film envelope)Variable (depends on film type)
Common in IndiaVery common — aluminium strips used extensivelyVery common — most modern pharmaceutical packaging

9.3 Tamper-Evident Packaging

Packaging designed to reveal evidence of tampering (opening before purchase). IP and regulatory authorities require tamper-evident packaging for most pharmaceutical products.

  • Induction seals — aluminium foil sealed across bottle mouth by electromagnetic induction; must be removed to open
  • Shrink wrap / bands — plastic shrink band around cap; breaks if twisted or opened
  • Breakaway caps — ring seals that break when first opened
  • Blister packs — inherently tamper-evident; individual blisters cannot be resealed after opening
  • Hermetically sealed ampoules — must be broken to open; cannot be resealed

10. Quick Revision Summary

📝 Key Points to Remember

  • Packaging functions: Protection, Containment, Identity, Patient Safety, Convenience
  • Container types (IP): Well-closed, Tightly-closed, Hermetically-sealed, Light-resistant, Child-resistant
  • Hermetically sealed = used for injectables and sterile products (ampoules, sealed vials)
  • Glass Type I (Borosilicate) = highest resistance = ALL parenteral (injectable) preparations — sterile use
  • Glass Type II = treated soda-lime = parenteral solutions (less sensitive)
  • Glass Type III = soda-lime = non-parenteral liquids, tablet/capsule bottles
  • Glass Type NP = general purpose = non-aqueous preparations, solids only
  • Amber glass = protects from light (photosensitive drugs: GTN, folic acid, riboflavin)
  • HDPE = rigid tablet/capsule bottles (most common solid dosage form container)
  • PVC = blister pack forming film; IV bags
  • PP = syringes; autoclavable containers
  • Plastic problems: Sorption (drug absorbed into plastic) and Migration (additives leach into product)
  • Butyl rubber = best rubber for pharmaceutical vial stoppers (best gas impermeability)
  • Cold-form blister (Alu-Alu) = maximum protection (zero WVTR, zero O₂ transmission)
  • Storage temperatures: Freezer = −20 to −10°C; Refrigerator = 2–8°C; Room temp = 15–25°C
  • Label must include: Name, Composition, Manufacturer, Batch no., Mfg date, Expiry date, Storage, Dose, License no., MRP
  • Schedule H = Rx only; Schedule X = Narcotic/psychotropic (strictest)
