Pharmaceutical Suspensions

Complete notes — definition, types, Stokes' Law, flocculation, suspending agents, formulation, stability, evaluation and official preparations.

1. Introduction & Definition

A pharmaceutical suspension is a coarse dispersion (heterogeneous, biphasic liquid dosage form) in which insoluble solid particles (dispersed phase) are distributed throughout a liquid vehicle (continuous phase). Suspensions are thermodynamically unstable and must be shaken before use.

Official Definition (IP)

Suspensions are preparations containing finely divided drug particles distributed somewhat uniformly throughout a vehicle in which the drug exhibits minimum solubility. Particle size: 0.5–5 µm.

1.1 Advantages of Suspensions

  • Suitable for drugs that are insoluble or poorly soluble in water (Chalk, Bismuth subnitrate, Zinc oxide)
  • Better chemical stability than solutions — drug not dissolved, less hydrolysis and oxidation
  • Taste masking — undissolved drug has less taste (e.g., Chloramphenicol palmitate)
  • Can give prolonged drug action — depot effect (Insulin zinc suspension, Procaine penicillin)
  • Suitable for drugs unstable in solution form
  • Flexible dosing — adjust dose by changing volume
  • Suitable for children and elderly who cannot swallow tablets

1.2 Disadvantages of Suspensions

  • Physical instability — particles settle on standing (sedimentation)
  • Caking — hard sediment difficult or impossible to redisperse
  • Dose uniformity difficult — unequal drug distribution
  • Must be shaken before use — patient compliance issue
  • Bulky — inconvenient to carry
  • Shorter shelf life than tablets or capsules

⭐ Why Flocculated is Preferred — Exam Important!

Flocculated suspensions settle faster (large flocs) but form a loose, porous sediment that is easily redispersed by gentle shaking. Deflocculated suspensions settle slowly but form a hard cake that cannot be redispersed — making them pharmaceutically unacceptable for use.

3. Theory — Sedimentation & Stokes' Law

Suspended particles denser than the liquid medium settle under gravity — this is sedimentation. The rate is described by Stokes' Law.

3.1 Stokes' Law (Must Know!)

📐 Stokes' Law Formula

V = d²(ρs − ρl) × g / 18η

V = settling velocity (cm/sec)
d = particle diameter (cm) — most critical factor (d²)
ρs = density of solid particles (g/mL)
ρl = density of liquid vehicle (g/mL)
g = gravitational acceleration (980 cm/sec²)
η = viscosity of dispersion medium (poise)

3.2 Methods to Reduce Sedimentation

Factor in Stokes' LawHow to Reduce VPharmaceutical Method
Particle size (d²)Reduce particle sizeMicronization, ball milling, trituration — MOST effective
Density difference (ρs−ρl)Reduce density differenceAdd glycerin, sucrose to increase vehicle density
Viscosity (η)Increase viscosity of vehicleAdd suspending agents (CMC, Tragacanth, Bentonite)

3.3 Zeta Potential and Stability

Zeta potential = electric potential at the slipping plane of particles. Measure of repulsive forces between particles.

  • >±30 mV = high repulsion = deflocculated (particles repel each other, settle slowly, risk of caking)
  • Near zero mV = low repulsion = flocculated (particles clump, settle fast, easy redispersion)
  • Controlled flocculation = intentionally reducing zeta potential to optimal level by adding electrolytes (NaCl, AlCl₃) — best approach for pharmaceutical suspensions

  • Carbomer (Carbopol) — 0.1–0.5%; highly efficient; must be neutralised with triethanolamine (TEA) to form gel/increase viscosity
  • Povidone (PVP) — 0.5–5%; thickening and stabilising agent

4.3 Wetting Agents

Hydrophobic drugs need wetting agents to reduce the contact angle so water can wet the drug surface:

  • Surfactants: Polysorbate 80 (Tween 80) — most used; Sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS); Poloxamer 188
  • Hydrophilic colloids: Acacia, Tragacanth — act as both wetting and suspending agents
  • Solvents as wetting aids: Glycerin, Propylene glycol (1–5%)

5. Preparation of Suspensions

5.1 Dispersion Method (Most Common — for hydrophilic drugs)

  1. Reduce particle size by trituration or micronization
  2. Prepare suspending agent mucilage (dissolve/swell in part of the vehicle)
  3. Triturate drug with a small quantity of vehicle → smooth paste
  4. Add suspending agent mucilage gradually with continuous stirring
  5. Add remaining ingredients (preservative, sweetener, colour, flavour, buffer)
  6. Make up to final volume with vehicle
  7. Homogenise; pass through colloid mill if needed
  8. Label: "Shake well before use"

5.2 Precipitation Method (for hydrophobic drugs or ultra-fine particles)

  1. Dissolve drug in water-miscible organic solvent (alcohol, acetone)
  2. Add drug solution slowly to aqueous vehicle containing wetting agent while stirring rapidly
  3. Drug precipitates as very fine particles (in situ precipitation)
  4. Add suspending agent, other excipients, make to volume

Example: Sulfur suspension — sulfur dissolved in alcohol, precipitated in water to give very fine particles.

5.3 Condensation Method (Chemical Reaction)

Drug formed in situ by chemical reaction. Example: Bismuth subnitrate suspension formed by reacting bismuth nitrate with NaOH.

6.1 Sedimentation Volume (F)

📐 Sedimentation Volume

F = Vu / Vo

Vu = ultimate (final settled) volume of sediment
Vo = original total volume of suspension

F = 1 → no settling at all (ideal, but practically difficult)
F close to 1 → more stable, better suspension
F low (near 0) → severe settling, hard cake possible

6.2 Degree of Flocculation (β)

📐 Degree of Flocculation

β = F / F∞

F = sedimentation volume of flocculated suspension
F∞ = sedimentation volume of deflocculated (ultimate) suspension
β > 1 → flocculated suspension preferred over deflocculated

10. Quick Revision Summary

📝 Key Points to Remember

  • Suspension = heterogeneous, biphasic, thermodynamically unstable
  • Particle size = 0.5–5 µm; must shake before use
  • Stokes' Law: V = d²(ρs−ρl)g / 18η — particle size is most critical factor (d²)
  • Flocculated = pharmaceutically preferred (loose cake, easy redispersion, no caking)
  • Deflocculated = elegant appearance but forms hard cake
  • Best natural suspending agent: Tragacanth
  • Best semisynthetic suspending agent: CMC (NaCMC)
  • Best wetting agent: Polysorbate 80 (Tween 80)
  • Flocculating agents: Electrolytes (reduce zeta potential → controlled flocculation)
  • Zeta potential >±30 mV = stable deflocculated suspension
  • Sedimentation volume F = 1 = ideal (no settling)
  • Degree of flocculation β = F/F∞ ; β > 1 = flocculated preferred
  • Main stability problem = Caking (prevented by controlled flocculation)
  • Parenteral suspensions = IM/SC ONLY — NEVER INTRAVENOUS (IV)
  • Calamine Lotion IP = Calamine + ZnO suspended using Bentonite + CMC
