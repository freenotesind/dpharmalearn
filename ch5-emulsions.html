Pharmaceutical Emulsions

Complete notes — definition, types, HLB system, emulsifying agents, formulation, preparation, stability, evaluation and official preparations.

1. Introduction & Definition

A pharmaceutical emulsion is a thermodynamically unstable, biphasic liquid dosage form in which two immiscible liquids (oil and water) are homogeneously mixed, with one liquid dispersed as droplets (globules) in the other, and stabilised by an emulsifying agent (emulsifier).

Key Definition

Emulsion = Oil + Water + Emulsifying Agent
Globule size: 0.1–100 µm. Always thermodynamically unstable — emulsifier provides kinetic stability only. Must be shaken before use.

1.1 Advantages of Emulsions

  • Allows administration of oily/immiscible drugs in a palatable form (e.g., castor oil, cod liver oil)
  • Taste masking — oily or bitter drugs masked in emulsion form
  • Better bioavailability — O/W emulsions improve absorption of poorly water-soluble drugs
  • Can be formulated for topical, oral or IV use
  • IV fat emulsions used for total parenteral nutrition (TPN)
  • W/O emulsions used as water-repellent protective creams
  • Can deliver large doses of oil-soluble drugs in small volume

1.2 Disadvantages of Emulsions

  • Thermodynamically unstable — will eventually separate (cracking)
  • Susceptible to microbial contamination — aqueous phase supports growth
  • Difficult to accurately dose if creaming occurs
  • Require skilled formulation — choice of correct emulsifier is critical
  • Bulky — inconvenient packaging and transport
  • Short shelf life compared to solid dosage forms

⭐ Most Reliable Tests — Exam Tip

Dilution test and Conductivity test are the most reliable and commonly asked. Remember: O/W = water is outside = dilutes with water = conducts electricity.

3. Emulsifying Agents (Emulsifiers)

Emulsifying agents are substances that reduce interfacial tension between oil and water and form a mechanical barrier film around dispersed globules preventing coalescence.

4. HLB System (Hydrophilic-Lipophilic Balance)

Introduced by W.C. Griffin (1949), the HLB value indicates the relative affinity of an emulsifier for oil and water. Scale: 0–20.

1–3Antifoaming agents—

4.2 Calculating HLB of Blended Emulsifiers

📐 Blended HLB Formula

HLB blend = (HLB₁ × fraction₁) + (HLB₂ × fraction₂)

Example: Mix 30% Span 80 (HLB 4.3) + 70% Tween 80 (HLB 15)
HLB blend = (4.3 × 0.3) + (15 × 0.7) = 1.29 + 10.5 = 11.79
This blend would be suitable for O/W emulsion of liquid paraffin (Required HLB = 12)

⭐ Tween + Span Blending — Exam Tip

Tweens (high HLB) and Spans (low HLB) are commonly blended to achieve the required HLB of any oil. This is a preferred approach in pharmaceutical practice because the blend gives better physical stability than a single emulsifier alone.

5. Preparation of Emulsions

5.1 Dry Gum Method (Continental Method) — MOST IMPORTANT

📐 4:2:1 Rule — Must Memorise!

Fixed oil (e.g., arachis oil, olive oil): Oil 4 : Water 2 : Gum (Acacia) 1
Volatile oil (e.g., peppermint oil): Oil 2 : Water 2 : Gum 1
Mineral oil (liquid paraffin): Oil 3 : Water 2 : Gum 1

Steps:

  1. Measure the correct proportions of oil and acacia (dry) — 4:1 ratio for fixed oils
  2. Place acacia in a dry mortar
  3. Add oil and triturate to coat the gum with oil
  4. Add the correct amount of water ALL AT ONCE (this is critical — creates primary emulsion with crackling sound)
  5. Triturate vigorously and continuously until a white, creamy primary emulsion is formed (crackling sound indicates O/W formation)
  6. Add remaining water in small portions while triturating
  7. Transfer to measure, add other ingredients (sweetener, preservative, flavour), make to final volume

💡 Why add water all at once?

Adding water ALL AT ONCE in the Dry Gum method ensures the correct water:gum:oil ratio for forming the primary O/W emulsion. If water is added in small portions, the gum swells unevenly and a W/O emulsion may form instead.

5.2 Wet Gum Method (English Method)

  1. Prepare acacia mucilage first — triturate acacia with the correct amount of water
  2. Add oil slowly, in small portions, while continuously trituration
  3. Each portion of oil emulsified before next is added
  4. Add remaining water, other ingredients, make to volume

Note: This method is slower but can be easier for beginners. The same 4:2:1 ratio applies.

5.3 Bottle Method (Forbes Bottle Method)

Used for volatile and non-viscous oils that are difficult to emulsify by trituration.

  1. Place dry acacia in a dry, wide-mouthed bottle
  2. Add oil and shake to mix
  3. Add water all at once and shake vigorously
  4. Primary emulsion forms; dilute with remaining vehicle

6. Instability of Emulsions

Instability ProblemDescriptionReversible?Prevention
CreamingGlobules migrate upward (O/W — oil less dense) or downward (W/O) forming a cream layer. No coalescence yet.✅ YES — can be redispersed by shakingReduce globule size; increase viscosity; use density-matched formula
FlocculationGlobules aggregate into loose clusters but retain their individual identities — no merging✅ YES — reversible by gentle shakingMaintain appropriate zeta potential
CoalescenceGlobules merge together forming larger droplets — interface film breaks❌ NO — irreversibleStrong emulsifier film; correct HLB emulsifier; avoid electrolytes
Breaking (Cracking)Complete and irreversible separation into two distinct layers — oil and water — emulsion is destroyed❌ NO — completely irreversibleProper emulsifier; avoid incompatible additives; controlled temperature
Phase inversionO/W emulsion converts to W/O or vice versaSometimes reversibleCorrect HLB emulsifier; avoid adding large amounts of dispersed phase
Ostwald ripeningSmaller droplets shrink and larger ones grow over time❌ Slow irreversible processNarrow droplet size distribution; viscosity enhancers; co-surfactants

⭐ Stability Order — Exam Tip

Creaming → Flocculation → Coalescence → Breaking
This is the sequence from least to most severe. Creaming and flocculation are reversible (early warning signs). Coalescence and Breaking are irreversible (emulsion destroyed). Preventing creaming and flocculation early prevents cracking later.

6.1 Preservation of Emulsions

Emulsions are susceptible to microbial growth because they contain water. Preservatives must be present in the aqueous phase at effective concentrations.

PreservativeConcentrationEmulsion Type
Methyl paraben (Methyl p-hydroxybenzoate)0.1–0.2%O/W and W/O
Propyl paraben (Propyl p-hydroxybenzoate)0.02%Used in combination with methyl paraben
Benzyl alcohol1–2%O/W
Benzalkonium chloride (BAC)0.01–0.02%O/W; cationic — incompatible with anionic emulsifiers
Chlorocresol0.1%O/W; used in topical emulsions

⚠️ Preservative Challenge: Partitioning

In O/W emulsions, preservatives may partition into the oil phase, reducing the effective concentration in the aqueous phase where microbes live. Therefore, the concentration used must account for this partitioning effect. Parabens are particularly susceptible to this problem.

10. Quick Revision Summary

📝 Key Points to Remember

  • Emulsion = Oil + Water + Emulsifier; globule size 0.1–100 µm
  • O/W = oil dispersed in water (milk, cod liver oil emulsion); W/O = water dispersed in oil (butter, cold cream)
  • O/W identification: Miscible with water; conducts electricity; water-soluble dye colours whole emulsion
  • HLB 3–6 = W/O emulsifier (Span); HLB 8–16 = O/W emulsifier (Tween)
  • Tween 80 HLB = 15; Span 80 HLB = 4.3
  • Best natural O/W emulsifier = Acacia; Best W/O = Lanolin (Wool fat)
  • IV emulsion emulsifier = Lecithin (from egg yolk/soya)
  • 4:2:1 rule for dry gum method (fixed oil = Oil:Water:Gum = 4:2:1)
  • Add water ALL AT ONCE in dry gum method (not slowly)
  • Instability: Creaming → Flocculation (reversible) → Coalescence → Breaking (irreversible)
  • Breaking = most serious — complete irreversible phase separation
  • Preservative partitioning into oil phase reduces effectiveness in aqueous phase
  • Stability test: Centrifuge at 3750 rpm for 5 hours — no phase separation = stable
