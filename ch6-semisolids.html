Semisolid Dosage Forms

Complete notes — ointments, creams, pastes, gels, jellies — bases, formulation, preparation, evaluation and official preparations.

1. Introduction & Definition

Semisolid dosage forms are preparations intended for topical application on the skin or mucous membranes. They have a plastic or gel-like consistency — neither fully liquid nor fully solid — and provide local or systemic drug effects through the skin.

Types of Semisolid Dosage Forms

  • Ointments — greasy, anhydrous or nearly anhydrous preparations
  • Creams — emulsion-based, O/W or W/O, semi-fluid consistency
  • Pastes — contain high proportion of finely powdered solids (>20%)
  • Gels / Jellies — transparent, aqueous or hydroalcoholic semisolids
  • Poultices — soft, moist, hot semisolid preparations applied externally

1.1 Advantages of Semisolid Dosage Forms

  • Direct local action at site of application — minimum systemic side effects
  • Suitable for skin diseases — dermatitis, eczema, fungal infections, inflammation
  • Can provide protective, emollient, or medicated effects
  • Avoid first-pass metabolism — drug absorbed transdermally bypasses liver
  • Easy to apply; patient compliance good
  • Can be formulated for prolonged action (e.g., transdermal patches)

2. Ointments

An ointment is a smooth, greasy semisolid preparation containing medicaments dissolved or dispersed in an ointment base, intended for external application to the skin or mucous membranes.

Ointments contain a low water content (<20%) and have an occlusive property — they prevent water loss from the skin (good for dry skin conditions).

2.1 Ointment Bases — Classification (Most Important)

Ointment bases are the vehicles that carry the drug. They must be: chemically inert, non-irritating, non-sensitising, stable, compatible with the drug, and easy to apply and remove.

Class 1: Hydrocarbon (Oleaginous) Bases

Most Important Base — Must Know!

White Soft Paraffin (White Petrolatum / Vaseline) — most widely used ointment base. Chemically inert, non-irritating, non-toxic, good occlusive/protective properties, does not support microbial growth, indefinite shelf life. Disadvantage: greasy, not water-washable, not cosmetically acceptable.

2.3 Preparation of Ointments

A. Fusion Method

  1. Melt ingredients in order of decreasing melting points (highest MP first)
  2. Heat on water bath — not direct flame
  3. Mix while cooling (stir continuously until congealed)
  4. Incorporate drugs at the lowest possible temperature to prevent degradation
  5. Water-soluble drugs dissolved in a small amount of water, then incorporated

Used for: Soft paraffin, cold cream, calamine ointment — bases that require melting to blend

B. Trituration Method (Incorporation Method)

  1. Drug powder reduced to fine powder by trituration in a mortar
  2. Add a small portion of the base, triturate to smooth paste
  3. Gradually incorporate more base using a spatula on ointment slab (tile or slab)
  4. Continue until all drug is evenly distributed throughout the base
  5. Pass through ointment mill (if needed) for industrial scale

Used for: Insoluble drugs in soft paraffin or semi-soft bases (e.g., Zinc ointment, Sulfur ointment)

3. Creams

Creams are semisolid emulsion preparations — either O/W or W/O type. They contain a significant amount of water (>20%) making them less occlusive and more cosmetically acceptable than ointments.

3.1 O/W Creams vs W/O Creams

PropertyO/W CreamW/O Cream
Continuous phaseWaterOil
FeelNon-greasy, light, pleasantGreasy, occlusive, oily
Water washableYes — easily removedNo — water repellent
Emulsifiers usedAnionic: Na/K soaps, SLS; Non-ionic: Tweens, Cetomacrogol; Cationic: CetrimideCa/Mg soaps, Spans, Lanolin, Beeswax
Drug releaseFast — good drug release into skinSlow — sustained drug release
Skin feelCool on application (water evaporates), cosmetically acceptableWarm, occlusive, moisturising
ExamplesVanishing cream, Cetrimide cream IP, Aqueous cream BPCold cream (Rose water ointment), Oily cream
Patient preferenceHigher — lighter feelLower — greasy

3.2 Preparation of Creams

Creams are prepared using the fusion method with two phases heated separately then combined:

  1. Heat the oil phase ingredients (fats, waxes, oils, oil-soluble emulsifier) to 70–75°C on water bath
  2. Heat the aqueous phase (water, water-soluble ingredients) to same temperature (70–75°C)
  3. Add aqueous phase to oil phase (or vice versa) with continuous stirring
  4. Stir continuously while cooling to room temperature (prevents phase separation on cooling)
  5. Add heat-labile drugs (preservatives, fragrances) at lower temperatures (<40°C)

4. Pastes

Pastes are stiff semisolid preparations containing a large proportion of finely powdered solids (20–50%) incorporated into a fatty ointment base or aqueous base. They are stiffer than ointments, do not melt at body temperature, and have better absorbing properties.

4.1 Properties of Pastes

  • Higher solid content (20–50%) than ointments — makes them stiffer
  • Do not melt at body temperature — suitable for weeping lesions (absorb exudate)
  • Less penetrating than ointments — act as surface protectants
  • Better adherence to skin — stay in place longer
  • Good absorbing and protective properties

5. Gels and Jellies

Gels are transparent or translucent semisolid systems consisting of a network of polymer chains swollen with liquid (usually water). They are aqueous (hydrogels) or hydroalcoholic in nature.

6. Poultices (Cataplasms)

Poultices are soft, moist, semi-solid preparations applied hot to the skin to provide heat, moisture and sometimes medication. They are applied by spreading on cloth and placed on the affected area.

  • Kaolin Poultice BP — Heavy kaolin + Boric acid + Peppermint oil + Thymol — used for chest congestion, boils, sprains
  • Linseed poultice — Linseed powder moistened with hot water — older preparation for boils
  • Applied hot to promote circulation, reduce inflammation, bring boils to head

10. Quick Revision Summary

📝 Key Points to Remember

  • Semisolids = Ointments, Creams, Pastes, Gels, Poultices — for topical application
  • Ointment bases: 1) Hydrocarbon 2) Absorption 3) W/O emulsion 4) Water-soluble
  • White soft paraffin (Petrolatum) = most commonly used ointment base
  • Lanolin (Wool fat) = best absorption base — absorbs 2× weight in water
  • PEG (Macrogol) = water-soluble base — non-greasy, water-washable
  • Fusion method = heat and mix ingredients; melt in order of decreasing melting points
  • Trituration method = incorporate drug powder into base on ointment slab
  • Cream = emulsion semisolid; O/W cream = non-greasy, washable; W/O = greasy
  • Paste = >20% solid powder in base; stiffer than ointment; does not melt at body temp
  • Zinc paste IP = ZnO 25% + Starch 25% + White soft paraffin 50%
  • Carbopol gel requires neutralisation with Triethanolamine (TEA)
  • Key evaluation tests: Spreadability, Penetrometry, Viscosity, pH, Drug release (Franz cell)
  • Skin pH = 4.5–6.5 — formulations should match this range
