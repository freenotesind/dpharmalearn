Powders & Granules

Complete notes — classification, size reduction equipment, sieve analysis, mixing methods, granulation techniques, flow properties and evaluation.

1. Introduction & Definition

A pharmaceutical powder is a solid dosage form consisting of one or more drugs in a dry, finely divided state. Powders may be used directly as a dosage form or as an intermediate in tablet and capsule manufacturing.

Key Definition

Powder = a dry, solid formulation in which particles are small and free-flowing. Particle size: typically 1 µm–2 mm. The art of reducing particle size is called Comminution.

1.1 Advantages of Powders

  • Greater chemical stability than liquid dosage forms
  • Rapid dissolution and absorption — large surface area (especially fine powders)
  • Flexible dosing — dose adjusted by measuring volume or weight
  • Suitable for drugs unstable in solution (antibiotics, dry syrups)
  • Easy to manufacture — simple equipment needed
  • Longer shelf life than liquids

1.2 Disadvantages of Powders

  • Difficult to mask bitter or unpleasant taste
  • Hygroscopic powders — absorb moisture and cake
  • Inaccurate dosing if powder segregates
  • Dusty — risk of occupational inhalation exposure during manufacturing
  • Poor patient compliance — difficult to carry and take
  • Some drugs irritate gastric mucosa as fine powder

⭐ Exam Tip: Sieve Numbers

Higher sieve number = smaller pore size = finer powder. Mnemonic: 10 → 22 → 44 → 85 → 120 (increasing number = finer). Most commonly asked: Fine powder = Sieve #85 (180 µm).

3. Size Reduction (Comminution)

Size reduction (comminution) is the process of reducing large particles into smaller ones. It increases the surface area of the drug, improving dissolution rate and absorption.

3.1 Objectives of Size Reduction

  • Increase surface area → faster dissolution and absorption
  • Improve uniformity of mixing — uniform particle size distributes evenly
  • Improve extraction efficiency of plant drugs
  • Improve physical stability of suspensions and emulsions
  • Facilitate drying of wet materials

Hammer Mill — Most Important Equipment

🔨 Hammer Mill

Principle: High-speed rotating hammers impact the drug particles. Particles break by impact and are reduced in size. A screen at the bottom controls the final particle size — only particles small enough pass through.

Advantages: Versatile; handles wide range of materials; continuous operation; easy to clean.
Disadvantages: Generates heat (not for heat-labile drugs); can cause electrostatic charge; not suitable for very hard or very sticky materials.

Ball Mill

⚙️ Ball Mill

Principle: A rotating cylinder containing grinding balls (steel or ceramic). As cylinder rotates, balls cascade and grind material by attrition and impact.
Optimal speed: 50–75% of critical speed. At critical speed, balls stick to wall (centrifuge effect) — no grinding.
Best for: Hard, brittle materials (minerals, crystals). Can be used for dry or wet grinding.

Fluid Energy Mill (Micronizer / Jet Mill)

💨 Fluid Energy Mill

Principle: Compressed air or steam injected at high velocity causes particles to collide at high speeds — particle-to-particle impact reduces size to 1–10 µm (micronization).
Advantages: No heat generated (cool process), no contamination from metal parts, suitable for heat-sensitive and sterile drugs.
Disadvantages: Expensive; high energy consumption.
Used for: Inhaled drugs (salbutamol, budesonide), sterile injectables.

4. Sieve Analysis (Particle Size Analysis)

Sieve analysis determines the particle size distribution of a powder by passing it through a series of sieves with decreasing pore sizes.

4.1 IP Sieve Analysis Method

  1. Arrange sieves in decreasing order of pore size (largest on top, smallest at bottom), with a receiver pan at the bottom
  2. Weigh a sample of powder and place on the top sieve
  3. Shake mechanically for a specified time (usually 5–10 minutes) at specified amplitude
  4. Weigh the amount of powder retained on each sieve
  5. Calculate % retained on each sieve and cumulative % passing

5. Mixing of Powders

Mixing (blending) is the process of bringing two or more components into close contact so they are randomly distributed. In pharmacy, it ensures uniform drug distribution throughout the powder mass.

5.1 Geometric Dilution Method (Most Important)

📐 Geometric Dilution — Must Know!

Used for: Mixing a small amount of potent drug with a large amount of diluent.

Method:
1. Take the potent drug in a mortar
2. Add an approximately equal volume of diluent — mix thoroughly
3. Add another equal portion of diluent — mix again
4. Continue doubling the amount added each time until all diluent is incorporated

Why this method? Ensures the potent drug is uniformly distributed throughout the entire diluent mass — prevents dangerous local high-concentration areas in the mixture.

6. Granulation

Granulation is the process of converting fine powder particles into larger agglomerates (granules) to improve flowability, compressibility, and prevent segregation. Granules are used in tablet and capsule manufacturing.

6.1 Objectives of Granulation

  • Improve flowability — granules flow better than fine powder
  • Improve compressibility for tablet manufacturing
  • Prevent segregation — granules have uniform composition
  • Reduce dustiness — less occupational hazard
  • Improve content uniformity of low-dose drugs
  • Control dissolution rate of the drug

6.2 Wet Granulation — Most Common Method

⚗️ Wet Granulation — Steps

  1. Dry mixing — Mix drug + diluent + disintegrant (intragranular) in blender
  2. Binder addition — Add binder solution (PVP in IPA / Starch paste) to form a wet mass
  3. Wet massing — Knead the wet mass to the right consistency (not too wet, not too dry)
  4. Wet screening — Pass wet mass through sieve #6 or #8 to form coarse granules
  5. Drying — Dry granules in tray oven (60°C) or Fluid Bed Dryer (FBD) at 40–60°C
  6. Dry screening — Sieve dried granules to uniform size (#14–#20)
  7. Lubrication — Mix with lubricant (Mg stearate) + glidant (Aerosil) — extragranular
  8. Compression or filling — Granules ready for tablet press or capsule filling

Dry mixingV-blender / Double cone blenderUniform distribution of drug and excipients

6.3 Dry Granulation (Slugging / Roller Compaction)

⚙️ Dry Granulation

Method 1 — Slugging:
Drug + excipients compressed into large tablets called slugs on a heavy-duty tablet press → slugs broken up by milling → granules screened to uniform size → lubricated → compressed into final tablets.

Method 2 — Roller Compaction:
Powder compressed between two rollers → compact ribbon of powder → broken into granules by mill.

Used for: Drugs sensitive to moisture (effervescent tablets) or heat (thermolabile drugs). No liquid binder, no drying step needed.

7. Flow Properties of Powders

Good powder flow is essential for accurate tablet filling and capsule filling. The following tests measure powder flowability:

7.1 Angle of Repose

📐 Angle of Repose

tan θ = h / r

θ = angle of repose; h = height of heap; r = radius of base

Powder poured through funnel onto surface forms a cone heap — angle measured.
<25° = excellent flow
25–30° = good flow
30–40° = fair (may cause problems)
>40° = poor flow (cohesive, will not flow well)

7.2 Bulk Density and Tapped Density

PropertyFormulaDefinition
Bulk density (ρb)ρb = Mass / Bulk volumeDensity of powder including air between particles — measured before tapping
Tapped density (ρt)ρt = Mass / Tapped volumeDensity after standard tapping (settled packing) — no air spaces

7.3 Carr's Index (Compressibility Index)

📐 Carr's Index (CI)

CI = [(ρt − ρb) / ρt] × 100

<15% = excellent compressibility and flow
15–20% = good
20–35% = fair
>35% = very poor flow (cohesive powder)

7.4 Hausner Ratio

📐 Hausner Ratio (HR)

HR = ρt / ρb (Tapped density / Bulk density)

HR <1.25 = good flow
HR 1.25–1.5 = fair flow
HR >1.5 = poor flow (cohesive)

Relationship: HR = 1 + CI/100 (approximately). Both Carr's index and Hausner ratio use the same density measurements.

10. Quick Revision Summary

📝 Key Points to Remember

  • Comminution = reducing particle size; improves dissolution and absorption
  • Hammer mill = most widely used in pharma industry
  • Fluid energy mill (micronizer) = finest output (1–10 µm); for sterile and inhalation drugs
  • Ball mill = attrition + impact; optimal speed 50–75% of critical speed
  • IP powder classification: Coarse (#10) → Moderately coarse (#22) → Moderately fine (#44) → Fine (#85) → Very fine (#120)
  • Fine powder = Sieve #85 = 180 µm
  • Geometric dilution = standard method for mixing potent drugs with diluent
  • Wet granulation = most common granulation method; 7–8 steps; uses liquid binder
  • Dry granulation (slugging) = for moisture/heat-sensitive drugs; no liquid binder
  • Angle of repose <25° = excellent flow; >40° = poor flow
  • Carr's index <15% = excellent; >35% = very poor
  • Hausner ratio <1.25 = good flow; >1.5 = poor flow
  • FBD (Fluid Bed Dryer) = best dryer for granules — fast, uniform drying
  • Effervescent granule ratio: Citric acid 5 + Tartaric acid 10 + NaHCO₃ 14
