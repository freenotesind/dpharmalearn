Tablets

Complete notes on types, excipients, manufacturing methods, tablet coating, quality control tests, defects and official preparations.

1. Introduction and Definition

Tablets are solid, unit dosage forms prepared by compressing or moulding drug with suitable excipients. They represent approximately 70% of all medicines manufactured worldwide and are the most popular oral dosage form.

IP Definition

Tablets are solid preparations each containing a single dose of one or more active substances, prepared by compressing uniform volumes of powder or granules. May contain diluents, binders, disintegrants, lubricants, glidants and coating agents as excipients.

1.1 Advantages of Tablets

  • Accurate and reproducible dose in every tablet
  • Best chemical stability among all oral dosage forms
  • Easy to mass produce — economical with consistent quality control
  • Convenient for patient — easy to carry, identify, and swallow
  • Can be modified for various release patterns — IR, SR, EC, sublingual
  • Taste masking possible with coating — improves compliance
  • Easy identification by shape, colour, and embossing
  • Long shelf life compared to liquid preparations

1.2 Disadvantages of Tablets

  • Difficult for patients who cannot swallow (young children, elderly, unconscious patients)
  • Some drugs have poor bioavailability in solid form — dissolution-limited
  • Drugs with poor compressibility are difficult to tablet
  • Drugs irritating to GI tract require special enteric coating
  • Hygroscopic drugs become sticky and are difficult to manufacture
  • Drugs needing very high doses (>1 g per dose) result in unacceptably large tablets

3. Tablet Excipients

Excipients are inactive substances added to ensure proper manufacturing, stability, appearance, and bioavailability of tablets.

3.1 Diluents (Fillers)

Added to increase the bulk so tablets can be formed at a practical size when drug dose is small.

DiluentSolubilityNotes
Lactose (most common)Water-solubleMonohydrate for wet granulation; anhydrous for direct compression; compatible with most drugs
MCC (Microcrystalline Cellulose / Avicel)InsolubleBest for direct compression; self-lubricating; excellent compressibility; also acts as disintegrant
Dicalcium Phosphate (DCP)InsolubleAnhydrous DCP ideal for direct compression; hard, alkaline
StarchInsolubleTraditional diluent; poor flow alone; also serves as disintegrant
MannitolWater-solubleCool sweet taste; used in chewable and ODT tablets
SorbitolWater-solubleSweet; diabetic-friendly; chewable tablets

3.2 Binders (Adhesives)

Hold particles together during granulation and compression, giving tablets mechanical strength.

BinderConcentrationNotes
Povidone (PVP)2-8%Most widely used binder; excellent wet and dry binding; broad compatibility; dissolves in water and alcohol
Starch paste5-20%Traditional binder; cooked maize or potato starch; most economical option
HPMC2-5%Good wet and dry binder; also used in film coating
Gelatin2-5%Hot aqueous solution used; excellent strength; traditional binder
Acacia solution3-5%Natural gum binder; good strength but microbial contamination risk

3.3 Disintegrants

Break tablet apart in gastric fluid, releasing drug for absorption.

DisintegrantConcentrationMechanismNotes
Starch5-20%Swelling + wickingOldest disintegrant; maize or potato starch
SSG (Sodium Starch Glycolate / Primojel)2-8%Super-swelling (200-300x volume)Superdisintegrant; most commonly used; rapid disintegration
Croscarmellose Sodium (Ac-Di-Sol)1-5%Swelling + wickingSuperdisintegrant; cross-linked CMC; excellent for direct compression
Crospovidone (Polyplasdone XL)2-5%Wicking/capillary actionSuperdisintegrant; cross-linked PVP; pH independent; no swelling
MCC (Avicel)5-15%WickingDual function: diluent + disintegrant

STAR: Superdisintegrants - Must Know for Exam!

Three superdisintegrants: 1) SSG (Sodium Starch Glycolate) - swells 200-300x; 2) Croscarmellose sodium - fibrous cross-linked CMC; 3) Crospovidone - cross-linked PVP, wicking mechanism. All used at 2-8%, far more powerful than plain starch.

3.4 Lubricants

Reduce friction between tablet mass and die wall during compression and ejection, preventing sticking.

LubricantConcentrationSolubilityNotes
Magnesium stearate0.25-1%InsolubleMost widely used lubricant; hydrophobic; excellent lubrication; can reduce dissolution if overused; incompatible with aspirin and vitamin C
Talc1-5%InsolubleHydrophobic; glidant + lubricant; used with Mg stearate
Stearic acid1-5%InsolubleGood lubricant; hydrophobic
PEG 4000/60002-5%SolubleWater-soluble lubricant - does not reduce dissolution; ideal for dispersible tablets
Sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS)1-2%SolubleWater-soluble; also wetting agent; good for dispersible and soluble tablets

3.5 Glidants and Coating Agents

Glidants improve powder/granule flow into the die:

  • Colloidal Silicon Dioxide (Aerosil / Cab-O-Sil) 0.1-0.5% - most effective glidant; adsorbs onto particles
  • Talc 1-5% - glidant + lubricant combination

Coating agents applied over tablet core: HPMC (film coat), CAP/Eudragit L (enteric), Sugar, Shellac

4. Manufacturing Methods

4.1 Wet Granulation - Most Common

Process Steps - Wet Granulation

Step 1: Weigh and sieve drug + diluent + intragranular disintegrant
Step 2: Dry mix all sieved ingredients in blender
Step 3: Prepare binder solution (PVP in water, or starch paste)
Step 4: Add binder to dry mix gradually to form wet mass (moist dough consistency)
Step 5: Pass wet mass through oscillating granulator or sieve #8 to form wet granules
Step 6: Dry granules in tray dryer (60-70 deg C) or Fluid Bed Dryer (FBD) to moisture 1-2%
Step 7: Size dry granules through sieve #20
Step 8: Add extragranular lubricant + remaining disintegrant, blend
Step 9: Compress on rotary tablet press
Step 10: QC tests (weight variation, hardness, friability, disintegration, dissolution)

Advantages: Best flow; best compressibility; strong tablets; uniform drug distribution; most versatile

Disadvantages: More steps; time-consuming; NOT suitable for moisture-sensitive or heat-labile drugs

Equipment: Planetary mixer, Rapid mixer granulator (RMG), Fluid Bed Granulator (FBG), Tray dryer, FBD, Oscillating granulator, Rotary tablet press

4.2 Dry Granulation (Slugging)

Process Steps - Dry Granulation

Step 1: Weigh and sieve all dry ingredients (drug + diluent + disintegrant + part lubricant)
Step 2: Mix all dry ingredients in blender
Step 3a - Slugging: Compress powder into large flat tablets called SLUGS (10-25 mm) on heavy-duty press
Step 3b - Roller Compaction (alternative): Feed powder between two rotating rollers under pressure to form ribbons/sheets
Step 4: Break slugs/ribbons into granules using oscillating granulator or hammer mill
Step 5: Sieve granules to required size
Step 6: Add remaining lubricant, blend
Step 7: Compress on tablet press

Advantages: No moisture used; no heat used; suitable for moisture-sensitive and heat-labile drugs; fewer steps than wet granulation

Disadvantages: Granule quality less uniform; more fines; heavy-duty equipment required; more dust generation

Suitable for: Aspirin, Vitamins B and C, effervescent tablets, hygroscopic drugs, heat-labile antibiotics

4.3 Direct Compression (DC)

Process Steps - Direct Compression

Step 1: Weigh and sieve drug + DC-grade excipients
Step 2: Mix all ingredients in blender (NO granulation step at all)
Step 3: Directly compress the blend on rotary tablet press

Requires DC-grade excipients with inherently good flow and compressibility:
Avicel PH-101/PH-102 (MCC), Anhydrous lactose, DCP dihydrate, Spray-dried lactose

Advantages: Simplest method; fewest processing steps; no moisture; no heat; fastest and most economical; best for moisture-sensitive and thermolabile drugs

Disadvantages: Drug must have good inherent flow and compressibility; limited drug loading (usually <30% of tablet); segregation risk if particle sizes differ greatly

Suitable for: Drugs with naturally good compressibility; potent drugs (small dose = easy DC); stable crystalline drugs

5. Tablet Coating

5.1 Sugar Coating (Older Method)

Multi-layer coating applied in rotating coating pan. Steps must be in order:

StepMaterial UsedPurpose
1. SealingShellac solution, Zein, CAP in organic solventSeals tablet core; prevents moisture entry; isolates tablet from sugar layers
2. Sub-coatingGum + CaCO3 paste or sugar + starch syrupRounds off edges; builds up bulk; fills surface irregularities
3. Smoothing (Grossing)Refined sugar syrupFills remaining irregularities; smooth surface; builds more bulk
4. ColouringSugar syrup + approved dye/lakeGives tablet its final colour for identification
5. PolishingCarnauba wax or beeswax in organic solventGives brilliant, shiny, glossy finish; protects colour

Disadvantages: Very time-consuming (several days for complete coating); 50-100% weight gain; skilled operator required; cannot be enteric without modification

5.2 Film Coating (Modern Method)

Thin polymer film applied by spray in rotating pan coater or fluid bed coater.

ComponentFunctionExamples
Film-forming polymerForms the film layer over tabletHPMC (most used), HPC, PVA (Opadry), HPMC + PEG blend
PlasticizerMakes film flexible (prevents cracking)PEG 400/6000, Triethyl citrate, Diethyl phthalate, Propylene glycol
SolventDissolves polymer for sprayingWater (aqueous) - preferred; Methanol, DCM (phased out)
Pigment/OpacifierColour and opacityTitanium dioxide (white), FD&C dyes, Iron oxides

Advantages: Fast (hours, not days); only 2-5% weight gain; can produce enteric or SR coatings; mechanically strong; aqueous coating is now standard

5.3 Enteric Coating - Exam Important!

Enteric coating resists gastric acid (pH 1-3) and dissolves only when pH >5.5 (intestine).

Why use enteric coating:

  • Drug irritates gastric mucosa (Aspirin, Diclofenac sodium, Naproxen)
  • Drug degraded by gastric acid (Omeprazole, Esomeprazole, Erythromycin)
  • Drug must act in intestine (some anthelmintics, local action in bowel)
  • Protect drug from stomach enzymes (Pancreatin, Digestive enzymes)
Enteric PolymerDissolving pHNotes
CAP (Cellulose Acetate Phthalate)>6.0Most commonly used; dissolves in small intestine; available as powder and aqueous dispersion
Eudragit L100>6.0Methacrylic acid copolymer; widely used; multiple grades available
Eudragit S100>7.0Dissolves in colon; used for colon-targeted drug delivery
HPMCP (HP-55)>5.5Hydroxypropyl methyl cellulose phthalate; good moisture resistance
PVAP (Polyvinyl acetate phthalate)>5.0Dissolves earliest; small intestine target
Shellac>7.0Natural resin; older enteric polymer; now rarely used

6. Quality Control Tests (IP Standards)

TestEquipmentIP Standard
Weight variationAnalytical balance - weigh 20 tablets individuallyNMT 2 tablets deviate from average: >5% (for >250 mg); >7.5% (80-249 mg); >10% (<80 mg)
Hardness (Crushing strength)Monsanto tester, Pfizer tester, Erweka testerStandard range 4-8 kg/cm2 (specification varies by product)
FriabilityRoche friabilator - 100 revolutions at 25 rpmNMT 1% weight loss
Disintegration timeIP disintegration apparatus - 6 tablets in water at 37 deg CUncoated: 15 min; Film-coated: 30 min; Sugar-coated: 60 min; EC: resistant in HCl 2 hr then disintegrates in pH 6.8 buffer within 60 min
DissolutionUSP Apparatus I (Basket, 100 rpm) or II (Paddle, 50-75 rpm) in 900 mL media at 37 deg CNLT Q+5% of drug dissolved (Q usually 75-80%)
Content uniformityAssay 10 individual tablets by UV spectrophotometry or HPLCEach tablet 85-115% of label claim; RSD not more than 6%
ThicknessVernier callipers or digital thickness gaugePlus or minus 5% of specified thickness

Friability Calculation - Must Know!

Formula: Friability (%) = (W1 - W2) / W1 x 100
W1 = Initial weight before testing; W2 = Final weight after 100 rotations

IP Limit: NMT 1% (Not More Than 1%)
Equipment: Roche friabilator, 25 rpm, 4 minutes (100 rotations)
If friability >1% = tablets too brittle = INCREASE binder concentration or compression force

7. Tablet Manufacturing Defects

DefectDescriptionCauseRemedy
CappingTop or bottom cap separates - horizontal crack at tablet edgeExcessive fines; too rapid decompression; air entrapment; deep concave punchReduce fines (<10%); slow decompression; adjust punch; pre-compression step
LaminationTablet splits into two or more horizontal layersOver-lubrication (excess Mg stearate); air between layers; too rapid compressionReduce lubricant amount; use slow compression; better deaeration
StickingTablet surface material adheres to punch faceWet granules (excess moisture); insufficient lubricant; rough punch surfaceDry granules properly (1-2% moisture); increase lubricant; polish and clean punches
PickingSmall amounts picked off by punch embossing designWet granules; punch design too deep; punch surface roughDry granules; modify punch design; polish punches
MottlingUneven colour distribution on tablet surfaceColoured drug migrates during drying; soluble dye migrates; uneven mixingUse lake dyes (insoluble); uniform mixing; controlled drying speed
ChippingTablet edges or surfaces chip offToo dry granules; excessive compression force; sharp die edgesAdjust granule moisture; reduce compression; check tooling for damage
CrackingSmall cracks on tablet surface or crownLarge granule size; tablet expands on ejection; springback too rapidReduce granule size; add more fines; modify punch
Binding in dieTablet sticks inside die - fails to eject cleanlyInsufficient lubricant; worn or damaged die; too much water-soluble materialIncrease lubricant; inspect and replace die; reformulate
Double impressionEmbossed logo appears twice (one faint, one clear)Punch rotates freely in die during tablet ejectionUse anti-rotation keys on punch; check punch-to-die fit

9. Quick Revision Summary

Key Points to Remember

  • Tablets = 70% of all medicines; most popular oral dosage form
  • GTN tablet = sublingual; Aspirin EC = enteric coated; Metformin SR = slow release
  • Diluent: Lactose (most common), MCC (best for direct compression)
  • Binder: PVP (most used modern binder), Starch paste (traditional)
  • Disintegrant: Starch (traditional); Superdisintegrants: SSG, Croscarmellose, Crospovidone
  • Lubricant: Magnesium stearate (most used, 0.25-1%)
  • Glidant: Aerosil (Colloidal SiO2) (most effective, 0.1-0.5%)
  • Wet granulation = most common; moisture + heat used; best granule quality
  • Dry granulation = slugging or roller compaction; no moisture/heat; moisture-sensitive drugs
  • Direct compression = simplest; fewest steps; requires DC-grade excipients
  • Film coating = HPMC polymer; 2-5% weight gain; fast
  • Sugar coating = 5 steps; 50-100% weight gain; time-consuming
  • Enteric coating: CAP most used; dissolves at pH >5.5
  • Friability: Roche friabilator; 100 rotations; NMT 1%
  • Disintegration: Uncoated 15 min; Sugar-coated 60 min; EC = 2hr acid resistant + 60 min buffer
  • Capping = top/bottom separation; Lamination = layer separation (both due to air/over-lubrication)
