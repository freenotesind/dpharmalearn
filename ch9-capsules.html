Capsules

Complete notes — hard gelatin capsules, soft gelatin capsules, sizes, shell composition, plasticizers, filling, defects, evaluation tests, storage and official preparations.

1. Introduction & Definition

Capsules are solid dosage forms in which the drug (with or without excipients) is enclosed in a hard or soft soluble shell made of gelatin (or other suitable polymer). The shell protects the drug and dissolves in gastric or intestinal fluid, releasing the drug for absorption.

Two Main Types

  • Hard Gelatin Capsules (HGC) — Two-piece (body + cap); filled with powders, granules, pellets
  • Soft Gelatin Capsules (SGC) — One-piece, hermetically sealed; filled with oils, liquids, semisolids

1.1 Advantages of Capsules

  • Excellent taste and odour masking — drug completely enclosed; patient never tastes bitter or foul drug
  • Easy to swallow — smooth, oval gelatin shell glides easily down throat
  • Elegant and attractive — can be printed and colour-coded for brand identification
  • Faster drug release than tablets — no compression binding; shell dissolves quickly releasing drug
  • Fewer excipients needed — unlike tablets, no binders or compression aids required for basic formulation
  • Suitable for drugs unstable when compressed (e.g., hygroscopic drugs)
  • Can be made enteric or sustained release using coated granules/pellets inside
  • SGC provides hermetic seal — ideal for volatile or oxygen-sensitive drugs
  • Better patient compliance — preferred over tablets especially by elderly

1.2 Disadvantages of Capsules

  • Cannot be halved or quartered — dose flexibility limited
  • SGC manufacturing requires specialised industrial equipment (rotary die machines)
  • Moisture sensitive — gelatin absorbs moisture; too much → soft/sticky; too little → brittle/cracked
  • Aqueous solutions cannot be filled in standard gelatin capsules — water dissolves shell
  • Drugs containing aldehydes are incompatible with gelatin (cross-link protein chains)
  • More expensive than equivalent tablet formulation
  • Lower mechanical strength — can be crushed or damaged if mishandled

2. Hard Gelatin Capsules (HGC)

HGC are two-piece rigid shells consisting of a body (longer, smaller diameter) and a cap (shorter, larger diameter). The body is filled with formulation, then the cap is pushed over the body to close it. They can be locked (pre-locked/fully locked) to prevent accidental opening.

2.1 Capsule Sizes — Complete Table (Must Know!)

⭐ Memory Rule: Larger Number = SMALLER Capsule!

Sizes range from 000 (largest, for big doses/supplements) to 5 (smallest, for paediatric tiny doses). For most pharmaceutical products, Size 0 is most commonly used. Memorise: the number goes up as the capsule gets smaller.

2.3 Manufacturing of HGC Shells — Dipping Process

  1. Gelatin solution preparation: Gelatin dissolved in hot purified water (60–70°C); colorants, opacifier added; solution filtered, degassed (to remove air bubbles that cause pin holes)
  2. Pin dipping: Arrays of stainless steel pins (mandrels) dipped into warm gelatin solution. Pin temperature and dipping time control shell wall thickness
  3. Rotation: Pins rotated after dipping to distribute gelatin evenly and prevent dripping
  4. Drying: Pins pass through temperature and humidity-controlled drying kilns. Controlled drying prevents cracking and ensures correct moisture content (12–16%)
  5. Stripping: Dried capsule shells stripped from pins by bronze stripping blades
  6. Trimming: Body and cap trimmed to specified lengths by precision cutting
  7. Joining (pre-locking): Cap placed loosely over body in pre-locked position for storage and filling
  8. Printing (optional): Branding or product information printed with food-grade ink

3. Soft Gelatin Capsules (SGC)

SGC are one-piece, hermetically sealed capsules with soft, pliable shells. The shell is made of gelatin + plasticizer (essential component). Fill is typically a liquid or semisolid. Manufacturing can only be done industrially (rotary die process).

3.1 Shell Composition — SGC

ComponentProportionFunction
GelatinMain ingredientShell structure — same as HGC
Plasticizer0.3–1.0 part per part dry gelatinMakes shell flexible and pliable; prevents brittleness
Water30–40% (more than HGC)Enables plasticity; higher water = softer shell during manufacture
Colorants and opacifierAs neededIdentification; light protection
PreservativesTraceMethyl/Propyl paraben — prevent microbial contamination of shell

3.2 Plasticizers — Most Important Component of SGC

⭐ Plasticizers Must Know!

Plasticizers make gelatin soft and pliable. Without them, gelatin forms a hard, brittle shell (like HGC). Amount determines softness — more plasticizer = softer shell.

Commonly used plasticizers:
• Glycerin (Glycerol) — most widely used; 0.3–1 part per part gelatin
• Sorbitol — used alone or combined with glycerin
• Propylene glycol — for low water content formulations
• PEG 400 — for special applications

Ratio: Dry gelatin : Plasticizer : Water = 1 : 0.4–0.6 : 1 (typical SGC shell)

3.3 Fill Materials for SGC

✅ Suitable Fills:

  • Fixed oils — Vitamin E, fish oil, arachis oil, cottonseed oil, soybean oil
  • Drug solutions in oils — Vitamin D in cottonseed oil; retinol in oil
  • Drug solutions in PEG 400 — water-miscible vehicle; very popular
  • Drug suspensions in oil or PEG — for insoluble drugs
  • Self-emulsifying drug delivery systems (SEDDS) — for poorly water-soluble drugs
  • Volatile oils — peppermint oil (enteric capsules), garlic oil

❌ NOT Suitable Fills:

  • Aqueous solutions — water migrates into gelatin shell → weakens and dissolves it
  • Aldehydes and aldehyde-releasing drugs — cross-link gelatin protein (formaldehyde, glutaraldehyde)
  • Strong acids (pH <3) or strong alkalies (pH >7.5) — hydrolyse gelatin
  • High concentrations of water-miscible solvents (ethanol >10%) — dissolve/soften shell

3.4 Manufacturing — Rotary Die Process

  1. Gelatin + plasticizer + water heated → form a homogeneous liquid gelatin mass
  2. Two continuous gelatin ribbons cast by spreader boxes onto chilled rotating drums (ribbon thickness controlled by drum gap)
  3. Ribbons fed to two counter-rotating die rolls with capsule-shaped pockets
  4. Fill material injected by a pump wedge between the two ribbons at the die nip point
  5. Die rolls simultaneously seal and cut capsules — both ribbons fused by heat/pressure at edges forming sealed unit
  6. Capsules washed (isopropanol), dried in tumble driers, inspected for defects, and packed

6. Common Capsule Defects & Remedies

DefectCauseRemedy
Telescoping (cap slides off body)Incorrect body-cap dimensions; high humidity during filling; over-lubrication of capsule exteriorCheck dimensional tolerances; control humidity during filling; reduce external lubricant
Denting / deformationToo high moisture (>16%); high storage temperature; excess moisture in fillControl storage RH to 40–60%; ensure fill is dry; use desiccant
Cracking / brittlenessToo low moisture (<12%); dry storage environment; excessive heatIncrease relative humidity in storage; ensure correct moisture during manufacturing
Sticking togetherToo much moisture; high temperature; contact with wet surfacesReduce humidity; lower storage temperature; add light dusting of talc
Pin holesAir bubbles in gelatin dipping solution during manufacturingDegas gelatin solution thoroughly before dipping; control dipping temperature and viscosity
Mottling / uneven colourUneven mixing of dye in gelatin solution; dye precipitationBetter mixing; filter gelatin solution; use soluble dyes
Poor body-cap lockMachine malfunction; lubricant on rim; moistureMachine maintenance; regular inspection; pre-lock all capsules after filling

8. Storage Conditions — Capsules

⭐ Ideal Storage — Must Remember!

Temperature: 15–25°C (room temperature)
Relative Humidity (RH): 40–60% — the most critical parameter
Container: Well-closed, airtight (HDPE bottles, blister packs)
Light: Protect from direct sunlight and strong artificial light
Odour: Away from strong-smelling chemicals — gelatin absorbs odours

Storage ErrorRH LevelEffect on Capsules
Too dry<40% RHShell loses moisture → becomes brittle and cracked; capsules break or split
Ideal40–60% RHShell maintains correct 12–16% moisture; physically stable; no sticking or cracking
Too humid>60% RHShell absorbs moisture → becomes soft, sticky, deformed; telescoping occurs; microbial growth risk
Very humid>75% RHCapsules stick together permanently; shell dissolves; microbial contamination

11. Quick Revision Summary

📝 Key Points to Remember

  • Capsules = drug enclosed in gelatin shell; HGC = 2-piece, SGC = 1-piece sealed
  • Sizes: 000 (largest) → 5 (smallest) — Larger number = SMALLER capsule
  • Size 0 = most commonly used pharmaceutical size
  • HGC shell: Gelatin + Water (12–16%) + Colorants + TiO₂ (opacifier) — NO plasticizer
  • SGC requires plasticizers (Glycerin or Sorbitol, 0.3–1 part/part gelatin)
  • HGC fills: Powders, granules, pellets — solid fills
  • SGC fills: Oils, oily solutions, PEG solutions — NOT aqueous solutions
  • SGC manufactured only by Rotary Die Process (industrial)
  • HGC disintegration: 30 minutes in water at 37°C
  • SGC disintegration: 60 minutes in water at 37°C
  • Storage: 15–25°C; 40–60% RH; airtight container; away from light and odours
  • <40% RH = brittle/cracked; >60% RH = soft/sticky/telescoping
  • HGC moisture: 13–16% — critical for stability
  • Aldehydes are incompatible with gelatin — cross-link the protein
  • Vegetarian alternative: HPMC capsules
  • Weight variation: NMT 2/20 capsules deviate >±10% of average fill weight
